The Menu

Two dishes.
Done properly.

Kim keeps the menu small on purpose — every bowl, every roll gets her full attention. The recipes come from her mother's kitchen in Sài Gòn; the herbs, more often than not, come from a balcony garden in Italy.

Signature · Beef

Phở Bò

— the soul of a Sài Gòn morning

Beef bones charred over open flame, then coaxed for sixteen hours with ginger, star anise, cassia, cloves and a single black cardamom pod. Served with hand-cut rice noodles, thin-shaved brisket, and a forest of herbs.

The Process

Three things
Kim refuses to rush.

Vietnamese cooking is mostly about restraint — the right ingredient at the right time, and then leaving it alone. Here's what that looks like in her kitchen.

01 / The Broth

Bones charred, never boiled hard.

Marrow bones blister over a flame until aromatic, then drop into water that's only ever allowed to whisper. A rolling boil clouds the broth — Kim won't have it.

02 / The Spice

Toasted in a dry pan, by smell.

Star anise, cassia bark, cloves, coriander seed, fennel — toasted until the kitchen tells her to stop. Bundled in muslin so the broth stays clear and bright.

03 / The Herbs

Cut at the last possible moment.

Thai basil, sawtooth coriander, mint, scallions, bird's-eye chili. Picked, washed, torn — never chopped — and added at the table so nothing goes flat.

Two Kitchens, One Cook

Where Sài Gòn
meets the Mediterranean.

Kim doesn't fuse the two cuisines — she lets them sit at the same table. Vietnamese technique, Italian pantry. A long simmer is a long simmer in any language.

About Kim

From a Sài Gòn alleyway to a kitchen in Italy.

Kim grew up in Ho Chi Minh City — learning to cook at her mother's elbow in a narrow kitchen that opened straight onto the street, where the smell of charring shallots blended with motorbike exhaust and the neighbour's coffee. She moved to Italy in her twenties for love, and stayed for the food.

"Italians and Vietnamese understand the same thing — that a good broth, a good ragù, a good anything takes all day. You can't be in a hurry."

Now she cooks somewhere between two grandmothers. Phở made with Piedmontese beef bones. Spring rolls finished with Ligurian basil when Thai basil's out of season. The technique is Vietnamese, but the pantry is half Italian — and somehow it works.
